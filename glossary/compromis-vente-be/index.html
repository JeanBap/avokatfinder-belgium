What is Compromis de Vente (Sales Agreement)?

Belgium's binding preliminary property contract — once signed, neither party can withdraw without penalty; the full transfer follows within 4 months before a notaire.

Key Facts — Compromis de Vente (Sales Agreement)
  • Binding preliminary contract — signed by both parties, creates irrevocable obligation
  • Includes purchase price, deposit, completion date, and conditions
  • Deposit typically 5–10%, held in escrow
  • Must include suspensive mortgage condition (buyer's escape clause)
  • Full transfer must occur before a notaire within 4 months

The compromis de vente (or "compromis van koop" in Dutch-speaking Flanders) is Belgium's binding preliminary property purchase agreement. Once signed by both buyer and seller, it creates an irrevocable legal obligation for both parties to complete the transaction. This is fundamentally different from some jurisdictions where preliminary agreements are conditional or can be easily cancelled. In Belgium, signing a compromis de vente is treated as a serious legal commitment, and withdrawal without valid grounds can result in loss of the buyer's deposit and damages claims from the seller.

The compromis de vente is typically prepared by the seller's notaire or a real estate agent acting as intermediary. It includes the property address and cadastral details, the agreed purchase price, the deposit amount (usually 5–10% of the price), the completion date (typically 1–4 months from signing), any conditions precedent (such as mortgage approval), and signatures of both parties. Unlike the notarial deed that follows, the compromis does not have to be signed before a notaire, though many Belgian notaires draft it. It is a private contract between buyer and seller.

Belgium law provides specific protections for buyers within the compromis framework. Most compromis include a suspensive condition (condition suspensive) making the buyer's obligation conditional on obtaining a mortgage within a defined period. If the buyer's mortgage application is denied within this period, the buyer can withdraw without penalty, and the deposit is returned. This is the primary "escape clause" for buyers; negotiate a clear mortgage condition and deadline before signing.

The deposit (arrhes) specified in the compromis is held in escrow, typically by the notaire or a designated third party. Once the compromis is signed, the buyer must pay this deposit within the agreed period (usually within a few days). The deposit is forfeited if the buyer withdraws without valid grounds (such as a failed mortgage condition). If the seller breaches the agreement, the buyer can demand return of the deposit plus damages. If the seller withdraws without valid cause, the buyer can claim damages (typically the value of the deposit times two under Belgian law).

After the compromis de vente is signed, the transaction must be completed (acte authentique) before a Belgian notaire within 4 months. During this period, the buyer's lawyer typically conducts due diligence: verifying title through the Land Registry, checking for mortgages or liens, investigating building permits and zoning compliance, arranging surveys or inspections, and arranging mortgage financing. The notaire conducts closing tasks: arranging payment, calculating and collecting transfer taxes (droits d'enregistrement), and registering the deed with the property register. The final signing before the notaire is the point at which legal ownership transfers.

Common Mistake: Signing a compromis de vente without a clear mortgage condition. If your mortgage application is denied and the compromis has no suspensive mortgage clause, you lose your deposit and may face damages claims. Ensure the agreement includes a detailed mortgage condition with a specific deadline.
Expert Tip: Before signing, have your lawyer review the compromis de vente and verify that the property description, price, and all conditions match your understanding. Many Belgian compromis are prepared by notaires or agents in French (or Dutch in Flanders); request a translation or summary if needed to ensure you understand all obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I cancel the compromis de vente after signing?

Only if a condition in the agreement is not met (e.g. mortgage denial, failed building inspection). Otherwise, cancellation without valid grounds results in loss of deposit and potential damages claims. The compromis is a binding legal contract.

What happens if the seller withdraws from the compromis de vente?

If the seller withdraws without valid grounds, the buyer can claim damages typically equivalent to twice the deposit amount under Belgian law. You can also pursue specific performance (forcing completion) through the courts, though this is time-consuming.

How long do I have to complete the purchase after signing the compromis?

Typically 1–4 months, depending on what is agreed in the compromis. Most transactions close within 4–8 weeks, but complex deals with renovations or other contingencies may negotiate longer periods. The final deadline should be clearly stated in the compromis.
