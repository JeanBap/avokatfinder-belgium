Belgium Legal Term

Précompte Immobilier

French/Dutch: Précompte Immobilier / Onroerende Voorheffing

Annual Belgian property tax levied on all real estate owners, based on the cadastral income (revenu cadastral). Rates vary by region and municipality, typically totalling 20–50× the indexed cadastral income.

TL;DRAnnual Belgian property tax levied on all real estate owners, based on the cadastral income (revenu cadastral). Rates vary by region and municipality, typically totalling 20–50× the indexed cadastral income.

The précompte immobilier (onroerende voorheffing in Dutch) is the annual property tax in Belgium, owed by the owner of the property on 1 January each year. It is calculated on the revenu cadastral (RC / kadastraal inkomen) — a notional annual rental income assigned to the property by the Cadastre and fixed in nominal terms since 1975, but indexed annually by the federal government.

The précompte is calculated as the indexed RC multiplied by a base rate of 1.25% (federal level) plus regional and municipal surcharges (centimes additionnels). Total effective rates typically range from 20× to 50× the indexed RC depending on location — with higher surcharges in cities. Brussels municipalities often charge among the highest centimes additionnels in Belgium.

For expat property owners in Belgium, the précompte is assessed and invoiced directly by the regional tax authority (Brussels: BERHB; Wallonia: SPFF; Flanders: Vlaamse Belastingdienst). Bills are typically issued in September and payable within two months. Non-resident owners must ensure the tax authority has a current address — a Belgian representative or your notaire's address can be used.

Several exemptions and reductions apply. New builds are exempt from précompte for the first five years of occupation. Properties owned by certain categories of disabled persons, low-income pensioners, or large families may qualify for reductions. In Flanders, the woonbonus reduction for primary residences was abolished from 2020 but some transitional provisions remain for pre-2020 mortgages.

When purchasing Belgian property, ask the notaire to provide the most recent précompte bill. This reveals the current annual tax cost and allows you to assess whether the RC is likely to be revised (Belgian RCs have not been fundamentally reassessed since 1975, and a general revaluation has been politically discussed for years — any future revaluation would significantly increase précompte for many properties).

⚠ Common MistakeConfusing the revenu cadastral (RC) shown in property listings with the actual annual précompte. The RC is the base figure — multiply it by the applicable coefficient (typically 35–50× in major cities) to get the real annual tax bill. Always ask for the last précompte invoice, not just the RC.
💡 Expert TipIf you buy a new or recently renovated Belgian property, verify whether the 5-year précompte exemption for new builds has been applied. This exemption must be claimed by filing a declaration with the Cadastre within 30 days of first occupation — it is not automatic, and many buyers miss it through ignorance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is précompte immobilier deductible from Belgian income taxes on rental income?

In Belgium, rental income from non-commercial property is typically taxed on the indexed RC rather than actual rents received, making the précompte partially offset by the low taxable base. For commercial or professional lettings, the précompte is a deductible expense against actual rental income.

What happens if I don't pay my précompte?

Unpaid précompte accrues penalty interest and the regional tax authority can pursue enforcement including charges on the property itself. Unlike some countries, the Belgian précompte obligation follows the owner (not the property), but persistent non-payment can ultimately result in enforcement action.

Will the revenu cadastral ever be recalculated?

A general revaluation of Belgian RCs (last set in 1975) has been discussed politically for decades. Any revaluation would significantly increase précompte for most Belgian properties, as the indexed 1975 values are well below current market rental values. Current (2026) projections suggest a phased revaluation is increasingly likely — buyers of undervalued properties should factor in potential future tax increases.
