Belgium Legal Term

Syndic

French/Dutch: Syndic / Syndicus

The professional manager of a Belgian apartment building (copropriété forcée), appointed by the general assembly of co-owners. Responsible for day-to-day management, accounts, and enforcement of building rules.

TL;DRThe professional manager of a Belgian apartment building (copropriété forcée), appointed by the general assembly of co-owners. Responsible for day-to-day management, accounts, and enforcement of building rules.

In Belgian apartment buildings, the syndic (syndicus in Dutch) is the professional managing agent appointed by the assemblée générale (general meeting) of co-owners to manage the building's common areas, financial accounts, and day-to-day administration. Every Belgian apartment building with multiple owners constitutes a copropriété forcée (gedwongen mede-eigendom) — mandatory co-ownership of common areas — requiring a syndic by law.

Belgian law (the Act of 2 June 2010 on co-ownership) specifies the syndic's responsibilities: maintaining the building's common parts, calling and minuting general meetings, managing the building's bank accounts, collecting service charges, enforcing the statutes of the building (acte de base and règlement de copropriété), and representing the co-ownership in legal proceedings. The syndic can be an individual owner (syndic non-professionnel) or a professional management company.

For expat apartment buyers, the syndic is your primary operational contact for everything relating to the building's common areas. All maintenance requests, building insurance queries, and service charge issues go through the syndic. In Belgium's bilingual capital Brussels, syndics typically operate in both French and Dutch — but in Flanders and Wallonia, expect communications in the regional language only.

Before purchasing a Belgian apartment, request from the current owner or the syndic: the last three years of general meeting minutes (procès-verbaux / notulen), the current annual budget, the fonds de réserve (reserve fund) balance, any outstanding works voted but not completed, and confirmation that all service charges are paid. Belgian law gives the syndic the right to claim unpaid charges from the new buyer for the current and two preceding years — a significant potential liability.

The syndicat (all co-owners collectively) holds an assemblée générale at least once per year to vote on budgets, major works, and syndic appointment. Belgian co-ownership law requires certain majorities for different decisions: ordinary maintenance typically requires simple majority; major works and rule changes require qualified majorities of 3/4 or 4/5 of all shares (quote-parts).

Belgian law holds new owners liable for the previous owner's unpaid charges for up to three years — a liability that is not always visible in the purchase price or mentioned by the seller.
💡 Expert TipAsk the syndic for a situation de compte — a statement of the current owner's account showing all paid and outstanding charges. This single document protects you from inheriting the seller's service charge arrears and is your right to request before any Belgian apartment purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I attend the assemblée générale as a new owner before the acte authentique?

No — you are not a co-owner until the acte authentique is signed and registered. However, the seller can grant you an observer status by attending themselves or giving you a proxy. After completion, you automatically receive meeting notices and can vote at the next assembly.

What is the fonds de réserve and how much should it hold?

The fonds de réserve (reservefonds) is the building's maintenance reserve. Belgian law requires co-ownerships to maintain a minimum reserve of 5% of the annual common charges. In practice, well-managed buildings hold significantly more. A zero or minimal reserve means future special levies for any major work.

Can the syndic take legal action without the co-owners' approval?

For urgent conservatory measures, yes — the syndic can act without assembly approval. For other legal proceedings, a general meeting vote is required. The syndic can also pursue individual co-owners for unpaid charges without a specific assembly mandate.
