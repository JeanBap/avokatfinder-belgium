Belgium Legal Term

Permis d'Urbanisme

French/Dutch: Permis d'Urbanisme / Stedenbouwkundige Vergunning

Belgian planning permission required for construction, extensions, demolition, or change of use. In Flanders, called an Omgevingsvergunning (environment permit) since 2017, combining planning and environmental approvals.

TL;DRBelgian planning permission required for construction, extensions, demolition, or change of use. In Flanders, called an Omgevingsvergunning (environment permit) since 2017, combining planning and environmental approvals.

The permis d'urbanisme (stedenbouwkundige vergunning in Dutch; omgevingsvergunning in Flanders since 2017) is the Belgian planning permission required for all significant construction, renovation, extension, or change of use of buildings. Belgium's federal structure means planning law is primarily a regional competence — the rules differ materially between Flanders (Omgevingsdecreet), Wallonia (CWATUPE/CoDT), and Brussels (COBAT).

In Flanders, the 2017 omgevingsvergunning merged planning permission and environmental permits into a single integrated procedure administered by the gemeente (municipality) or the provincial authority for larger projects. Applications are submitted electronically via the Omgevingsloket. The permit integrates assessments of land use, construction specifications, and environmental impact in a single decision.

Before purchasing any Belgian property with renovation plans, the buyer should request a renseignement urbanistique (stedenbouwkundig uittreksel in Flanders) from the relevant municipality. This free or low-cost document reveals: the property's zoning classification, any existing permits on the property, any outstanding violations (infractions urbanistiques / stedenbouwkundige overtredingen), and the applicable planning rules. Existing violations can significantly complicate or preclude future sales.

Planning violations (infractions d'urbanisme / stedenbouwkundige overtredingen) in Belgium do not expire and are not subject to prescription in the same way as in France or Italy. In Flanders particularly, the municipality can order demolition of unlicensed construction even decades after it was built. This makes pre-purchase planning due diligence critical — any unauthorised works the seller undertook remain a liability for future owners.

For expat buyers undertaking renovations, the Belgian permit process varies in duration: simple permits for minor works take 60–75 days; larger projects go through a public inquiry (enquête publique / openbaar onderzoek) adding 30 days. Third parties (neighbours, environmental groups) have rights to object, and appeals can extend timelines significantly in contested cases.

It shows all submitted and granted omgevingsvergunningen for any Flemish address — a 5-minute check that reveals the planning history and any outstanding issues.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a Belgian planning permit expire if I don't build?

Yes — most Belgian planning permits have a validity period (typically 2–5 years) within which construction must commence. If you purchase a property with an existing permit and want to build under it, check the expiry date carefully — an expired permit has no legal effect and you must apply afresh.

Is there a simplified procedure for minor works?

Yes — in all three regions, certain minor works (small extensions, internal modifications, garden structures below a threshold size) are either entirely exempt or subject to a simplified notification procedure rather than a full permit application. Your municipality's website details what is exempt in your specific zone.

What is a certificat d'urbanisme / stedenbouwkundig attest?

A certificat d'urbanisme (attest) is a pre-application certificate that tells you whether a proposed development is in principle permissible under the applicable planning rules. It is not a permit, but it provides legal certainty before investing in detailed plans and a full application.
