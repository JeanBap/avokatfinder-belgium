Belgium Legal Term

Droits d'Enregistrement

French/Dutch: Droits d'Enregistrement / Registratierechten

Belgian property transfer taxes — the main acquisition cost for buyers. Rates vary by region: 12.5% in Wallonia and Brussels, 3% in Flanders for primary residences (klein beschrijf), 12% for others.

TL;DRBelgian property transfer taxes — the main acquisition cost for buyers. Rates vary by region: 12.5% in Wallonia and Brussels, 3% in Flanders for primary residences (klein beschrijf), 12% for others.

Droits d'enregistrement (registration duties) are the primary property transfer taxes in Belgium, collected at the time of the notarial deed. Belgium's federal structure means rates differ significantly by region — one of the most important factors in Belgian property acquisition cost calculations.

In Flanders (Vlaams Gewest), the standard rate is 12% but is reduced to 3% for the purchase of a primary residence (hoofdverblijfplaats) under the klein beschrijf regime, subject to conditions including establishing residency within 3 years and not selling within 3 years of purchase. A further abattement (exemption on the first €200,000 of the purchase price) reduces the tax base for qualifying buyers.

In the Brussels Capital Region and Wallonia, the standard rate is 12.5%, with reductions available for primary residences under local abattement schemes. Brussels offers an abattement on the first €200,000 for qualifying primary residence buyers, effectively reducing the tax on that portion to zero. Wallonia offers a similar mechanism with regional conditions.

For expat investors purchasing Belgian property as a second home, buy-to-let, or through a company, the full standard rate (12% in Flanders, 12.5% in Brussels/Wallonia) applies with no reductions. This makes Belgium one of the more expensive European entry markets for investment property — factor acquisition tax into yield calculations from the outset.

The droits d'enregistrement are collected by the notaire at completion and remitted to the regional tax authority. They are calculated on the higher of the agreed purchase price and the venale waarde (market value assessed by the tax authority). If the authority believes the declared price is below market value, it can assess additional duties — making under-declaration risky.

⚠ Common MistakeAssuming the Flemish 3% klein beschrijf rate applies automatically. You must establish primary residence in the property within 3 years and maintain it for a further 3 years. Failure to meet these conditions triggers clawback of the duty saving plus interest and penalties.
💡 Expert TipIf purchasing in Flanders and planning to use the property as your primary residence, confirm with your notaire that you meet all klein beschrijf conditions before signing the compromis. The conditions include income thresholds in some municipalities and property value limits — check these details early to avoid a surprise at the deed stage.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are droits d'enregistrement due on new-build properties?

New-build purchases from a developer (within two years of first occupation) are subject to TVA/BTW (VAT) at 21% instead of registration duties. This typically makes new-builds more expensive from a tax perspective, though some developers include VAT in their stated prices.

Can the droits d'enregistrement be rolled into my Belgian mortgage?

Most Belgian lenders allow you to borrow up to 90% of the property value for primary residences, but acquisition costs (including droits d'enregistrement and notaire fees) are generally not financed. Plan for 15–20% in cash to cover purchase costs above the mortgage amount.

What is the difference between droits d'enregistrement and the notaire's fees?

Droits d'enregistrement are a government tax, collected by the notaire on behalf of the regional authority. Notaire fees (honoraires) are the notaire's own professional charge, also set by a regulated tariff. Both are payable at completion but are distinct costs.
